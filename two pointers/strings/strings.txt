To build the largest possible string, we use a **Greedy strategy**: at every step, we want to pick the 
character that contributes to the lexicographically largest remaining sequence. The critical logic 
happens when we face a tie (e.g., both current characters are 'a'); we cannot choose randomly. Instead,

**Your Code (Dynamic Sliding Window):**
Your approach treats the problem like a **balancing act combined with a sliding window**. You maintain a "balance" variable (`zero`) 
where `0`s add weight and `1`s subtract it. The thought process is: "As I move `i` forward, I try to 'spend' my previous surplus 
characters to form pairs. If I run out of surplus (e.g., I have too many `1`s and not enough `0`s), I must move my left anchor `j` 
forward to discard old, unusable characters until the window is valid again." It is a mechanical, index-by-index simulation of trying 
to pair characters while manually adjusting the window size to keep the counts balanced.

**Standard Optimal Code (Group Comparison):**
The standard approach changes the perspective from **individual characters** to **blocks of characters**. The insight is that a binary 
string is just alternating groups, like "000" (size 3) followed by "11" (size 2). The thought process is: "I don't need to match indices 
manually. If I have a group of three `0`s followed by two `1`s, I strictly know I can form exactly 2 valid substrings (`01` and `0011`).
" Instead of managing a window, it simply remembers the *length* of the previous group and compares it to the *length* of the current 
group; every time the current group grows, if it's still smaller than or equal to the previous group, you automatically have a new valid 
substring.
